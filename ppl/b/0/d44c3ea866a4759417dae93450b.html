Narrative

Story

Connecting Context:
Daughter Eleanor (Nell) Talbot's birth certificate shows her parents as Richard Hays Talbot (a bootmaker) and Anna Maria Smith from Mackay.

Richard (Hayes) William Talbot [Note 1] was born in Hammersmith, England, to Philip Hayes Talbot and Elizabeth Ann Walton. He was baptised at St. Paul's, Hammersmith on 10/6/1840. His father was from Stowe-nine-churches in Northamptonshire, and his mother was from Buckingham (about 19 miles apart). They had their first child in 1836 (at Brentford, Middlesex) and were then married in Hammersmith in 1837. Richard was the 3rd of 10 children (8 of whom survived past childhood).

The 1841 England census shows Richard, aged 1, living with his father (a shoemaker), mother, and 4yo sister Elizabeth in Lower Mall, Hammersmith, Middlesex. Ten years later, the 1851 England census shows the family (Philip and Elizabeth, Richard now aged 11 and listed as a scholar, five siblings, and 2 lodgers) living at No. 8, Brook Green Lane, Hammersmith. And then finally, in the 1861 England census Richard's parents are living at 37 Dartmoor St (now known as Hillgate Place), Kensington [Note 2]. Richard is however lodging nearby at 55 Dartmoor Street with the family of Joseph Harris. Both Joseph (aged 37) and Richard (now aged 21) are listed as shoemakers.

The following year, in 1862, Richard, his parents, and his 6 younger siblings, all emigrated to Australia, aboard the "City of Brisbane" (one of the first private immigration vessels) with Richard's father, Phillip, being employed as a cook on the ship. The ship left Gravesend on 21/2/1862, and then Plymouth on 25/2/1862. The ship travelled around the south of Australia and up the east coast, arriving in Moreton Bay (Brisbane) on 28/6/1862. The ship was quite contentious for a number of reasons. Firstly, it was believed that the immigrants were headed to Maryborough and when it did not arrive there was considerable consternation.

More significantly, there were allegations of "immoralities", drunkenness, and other problems during the voyage, resulting in an inquiry being held by the Immigration Board after the ship arrived, at which Richard and both his parents were witnesses. The inquiry included allegations against Philip: "It has been clearly proved also that the cook, Talbot, whose family seem to have been highly favored by the Master - was permitted to exact fees, - to the manifest injury of those who could not, or would not, submit to such exaction" (p46). There were also allegations against Elizabeth: "Thereby further solemnly ...lure that I have seen Mrs Talbot more the once the worse for liquor - she was well known on board as a woman of low and intemperate habits - she used to frequent the Cabin called the Bush, in which two of her daughters had berths, and she was perfectly aware that three men had constant access to this cabin both night & day"  (note: presumably this is referring to Sarah, 19 y.o, and Ellen 15 y.o.). Finally there is a side note that indicates that Richard might have been employed as an assistant to the Purser: "Was there a Mr Talbot on board? There was. - A passenger? Yes. -Married or single? Single - he assisted the Purser".

By the time the family arrived in Brisbane the population of Queensland was approximately 34,300. It is unclear where Richard lived for the next few years, though an obituary for is father stated: "The deceased arrived with his family in Brisbane in 1862, and proceeded to Yamala, on the Peak Downs, where he remained for 12 months. He removed thence to Yaamba, where he carried on business for six years, and he then settled at St. Lawrence, where he has resided since". It is unknown how much of this applied also to Richard, though given that the marriage certificate in 1863 (see below) indicates that Richard was living in Yaamba, it is likely that he lived there until he moved to Mackay in 1869.

On 14/7/1863, around 1 year after arriving in Qld, Richard married Maria Anne Smith in Rockhampton. Maria had arrived in Qld on the same ship as Richard, though it is unclear whether they knew each other before leaving England. The marriage certificate shows them both living in Yaamba, with Richard a shoemaker and Maria a milliner. Their ages are not given.

Over the next 16 years Richard and Maria had 8 children: Louisa (31/3/1864), Edward (14/12/1865), Richard (2/7/1867), Charles (22/8/1869) [Note 3], Isabella (7/9/1871), Elizabeth (25/9/1873), Eleanor (1877) and Caroline (10/7/1879).

A 1917 Newspaper article, (describing Richard's move to Sydney) indicated that he had been living in Mackay for 48 years. This would put his arrival in Mackay at ~1869 (possibly coinciding with Richard's fathers move from Yaamba to St Laurence. Note that Mackay was only first settled seven years earlier in ~1862 (see http://www.mackayhistory.org/research/history/00_short_history.html).

An 1875 newspaper item indicates that Richard Talbot, bootmaker, Mackay, was offering a £1 reward for a lost mare. Two years later in 1877, a newspaper article lists Richard as a member of the School Committee of the State School at Mackay.

In 1878, Richard applied to take over the licence for the Steam Packet Hotel in Mackay. He then took over this hotel for a relatively short period from 1879 to 1880. On St Patricks day 1879, a poem published in the local Newspaper refers to Richard: "There's sturdy R. H. Talbot, you can see his duster wave". A few months later, another newspaper entry refers to the birth of his last daughter, Caroline, born at the Steam Packet Hotel. And then in late 1879, another article refers to a court case where a Joseph Bell was convicted of providing Richard with a £6 cheque under false pretences.

By July 1882, things appear to have deteriorated for Richard, and he was declared insolvent. By November the same year, Richard was advertising for work: "R.H. Talbot, Bookmaker, is to be found Next to the River View Hotel. All orders punctually executed either in making or repairs". He may well have rebuilt over the next decade, as there is an article from 1891 indicating that "there passed on 25th [July] seventy-five bullocks from Redcliffe to Rockhampton, the property of R.H. Talbot". In 1905, he is described as one of 7 shareholders in the North Queensland Meat Export Co., Ltd.

On 11/9/1917 (when Richard was 77 years old) a newspaper article stated "Mr. and Mrs R.H. Talbot, who have been residents of Mackey for 48 years past, intend leaving by the S.S. Bingera on Tuesday for Sydney, where they will in future reside". Richard then died around 6 years later, on 8/7/1923 at the age of 83, at Sacred Heart Hospice, Darlinghurst. He is buried at Rookwood Cemetery (section 7, row 20, grave 819).

His wife Maria lived another 5 years, dying in Brisbane, on 28/7/1928 at the age of 85.

Note 1:
In 1838 Richard's parents, Philip and Elizabeth had a child that they named Richard Hayes Talbot (Hayes being the paternal Grandmother's maiden name). Richard was baptised at St Paul's, Hammersmith on 26/8/1838, but then died 11 weeks later on 11/11/1838. When Philip and Elizabeth had their next child, he was listed on the baptism record (on 10/6/1840) as Richard William. In later life this second Richard appears to have used the name Richard Hayes Talbot (possibly because Hayes was regularly used by his siblings as a middle name).

See http://hidden-london.com/gazetteer/hillgate-village/

Note 3:
Anecdotal information (from Joan Lowe) indicates that Charles ended up living on a plantation on New Guinea.
