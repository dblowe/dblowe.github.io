Narrative

Story

Connecting Context:
Baptism for Richard Talbot lists his father as Philip Talbot. The relationship is shown in numerous other record - immigration and then various UK census records.

Philip Hayes Talbot was born in Stowe-nine-churches in Northamptonshire on 3rd March 1817 to Joseph Talbot and Martha Hayes. Philip's parents were both from Northamptonshire: his father, Joseph Talbot, born in Eydon in 1784 and his mother, Martha Hayes, born in Thorpe Malsor in 1795 (around 30 miles apart).

Details of Joseph and Martha's marriage have not been able to be located [Note 1], but on 26th March 1815 they had two daughters baptised in Rotherthorpe, Northamptonshire: Elizabeth and Mary Anne. Philip was then born on 3/3/1817 and baptised on 4th Jan 1818 in St Michaels, Stowe-nine-churches (6 miles from Rotherthorpe) [Note 2].

When Philip was only 15 months old his mother Martha died (buried on 18/4/1819, in Stowe-nine-churches), and then 4 years later, on 11/2/1823 still in Stowe-nine-churches, his father Joseph remarried to Anne Bree (or Norton). Joseph and Anne had further children: Edwin (1824), William (1826), Levi (1828), Emma (1830), Emily (1831), and Martha (1832), and then possibly Sarah (1839) and Jane (1842) [Note 3]. Joseph and Anne remained in Stowe until at least the 1861 census.

At some point before he was 20 years old Philip moved from Stowe to London. On 16/11/1836 Philip and Elizabeth (Bessie) Anne Walton (both aged ~20) had a daughter Elizabeth born on 16/11/1836 and baptised in Isleworth, London, on 26/2/1837. Philip and Elizabeth were then married in 1837 in St. Pauls, Hammersmith, though the exact date is not known [Note 4]. Elizabeth was born in Buckingham, but her parents were married in Byfield (where her mother was born) - just a few miles from Eydon, the home town of Philip's father, which would explain the connection.

Philip and Elizabeth appear to have settled in the Hammersmith area for the next 25 years, and had another 9 children, with 7 surviving past childhood (in the following, where necessary the year of birth is estimated from census/immigration records):

- Elizabeth: b. 16/11/1836, bap. 26/2/1837 Isleworth, London
- Richard Hayes: b. ~1838; bap. 26/8/1838 at St Pauls, Hammersmith; buried 11/11/1838 at St. Pauls.
- Richard William: b. 1839; bap. 10/6/1840 at St Pauls
- Sarah Ann: b. 1842; bap. 7/7/1844 at St. Pauls
- Isabella Kitty: b. 1/3/1844; bap. 7/7/1844 at St Pauls; d. ~1855
- Eleanor: b. 21/3/1846; bap. 4/2/1848 at St. Pauls
- Edward: b. 6/1/1848; bap. 4/2/1848 at St. Pauls
- Mary Anne: b. 30/6/1851; bap. 20/7/1851 at St. Pauls
- Hannah (Anne): b. 4/3/1853; bap. 27/3/1853 at St Pauls
- Isabella: b. 12/7/1857; bap. 2/8/1857 at Byfield, Northamptonshire

Note that in several cases the death of a child (Richard, Isabella) led to a subsequent child being given the same or similar name.

The 1841 census lists Philip (20yo, Shoemaker) and Elizabeth (20yo), and two children, Elizabeth (4yo) and Richard (1yo), living in Lower Mall, Hammersmith, Middlesex. Ten years later, the 1851 England census shows the family living at No. 8, Brook Green Lane, Hammersmith: Philip (34yo, Shoemaker, b. Stowe) and Elizabeth (33yo, wife, b. Buckingham) and the two original children as well as 4 more children: Elizabeth (13yo, House servant), Richard (11yo, scholar), Sarah (9yo, scholar), Isabella (7yo, scholar), Eleanor (5yo, scholar), and Edward (3yo, scholar). There is also lodgers: Thomas Webb (66yo, shoemaker) and John Odell (24yo, boot closer).

There is a possibility that during the mid 1850's Philip fought in the Crimean War. His obituary 40 years later describes him as a Crimean veteran, though no record can be found of his name in those who served in the war.

In the 1861 England census the family is living at 37 Dartmoor St (now known as Hillgate Place), Kensington [Note 5], this time with Philip (44yo, Shoemaker) and Elizabeth (43yo) and just the youngest of the 6 children from 1851, but three more children: Edward (13yo), Mary (10yo), Anne (8yo) and Isabella (4yo). Their son Richard (now 21yo) is lodging nearby at 55 Dartmoor Street with the family of Joseph Harris, and both are listed as shoemakers. Sarah (19yo) is working as a servant for the Taylor family at 10 Clarendon Villas. No record has been found for Elizabeth, Isabella and Eleanor.

The following year, in 1862, Philip and Elizabeth (now 45yo and 44yo) emigrated to Australia along with all of their living children (now aged from 22yo down to 4yo) except the oldest, Elizabeth (who married and emigrated separately). The emigration was aboard the "City of Brisbane" (one of the first private immigration vessels) and Philip was employed as a cook on the ship. The ship left Gravesend on 21/2/1862, and then Plymouth on 25/2/1862. The ship travelled around southern Australia and up the east coast, arriving in Moreton Bay (Brisbane) on 28/6/1862. The ship was quite contentious for a number of reasons. Firstly, it was believed that the immigrants were headed to Maryborough and when it did not arrive there was considerable consternation.

More significantly, there were allegations of "immoralities", drunkenness, and other problems during the voyage, resulting in an inquiry being held by the Immigration Board after the ship arrived, at which Philip, Elizabeth and their son Richard were witnesses. The inquiry included allegations against Philip: "It has been clearly proved also that the cook, Talbot, whose family seem to have been highly favored by the Master - was permitted to exact fees, - to the manifest injury of those who could not, or would not, submit to such exaction" (p46). There were also allegations against Elizabeth: "Thereby further solemnly ...lure that I have seen Mrs Talbot more the once the worse for liquor - she was well known on board as a woman of low and intemperate habits - she used to frequent the Cabin called the Bush, in which two of her daughters had berths, and she was perfectly aware that three men had constant access to this cabin both night & day"  (note: presumably this is referring to Sarah, 19 y.o, and Ellen 15 y.o.). Finally there is a side note that indicates that Richard might have been employed as an assistant to the Purser: "Was there a Mr Talbot on board? There was. - A passenger? Yes. -Married or single? Single - he assisted the Purser".

By the time the family arrived in Brisbane the population of Queensland was approximately 34,300. A later obituary described Philip's next few years: "… arrived in Brisbane with his family in 1862 and proceeded to Yamala, on the Peak Downs, where he remained for 12 months. He removed thence to Yaamba, where he carried on business for six years, and he then settled at St. Lawrence, where he has resided since".

Philip had two land orders [Note 6]. The first was issued on 24/7/1862 for £18. The second was issued on 27/8/1864 for £60 and appears to have been used in selecting land at St. Laurence in 1868 (by which time Philip was 51yo). It is likely this might have been at Byfield, given the reference in Philip's obituary about being from Byfield [Note 7].

There is also a record of a Philip Talbot as a Justice of the Peace in Qld in 1890.

In 1888 Philip's wife Elizabeth died and then three years later Philip died on 16/1/1891 at the age of 74 in Springsure, Qld (300km inland from St Lawrence), though he had been living in Byfield, St Lawrence. His death certificate gives his parents as Joseph Talbot and Mary Elizabeth Hayes, though the latter is probably a misrepresentation by whoever registered the death (or maybe Philip himself misremembered the name, given that his mother died when he was only 1 year old).

Philip was buried at Springsure cemetery - where his daughter Elizabeth (i.e. Mrs J.H.Hoare) was living. His estate of £664 was managed by his son-in-law William Ussher (married to Sarah).

Note 1:
There is a gap in the Thorpe Malsor marriage records between late 1811 and 1814, and given the baptism of Joseph and Martha's first children in Rothersthorpe in early 1815 it is quite possible they were married in 1812-1813 in Thorpe Malsor.

Note 2:
The only mention of Philips date of birth is in an obituary on his death, though this is consistent with his age on numerous census records (34yo in 1851, 44yo in 1861), immigration records (44yo in mid-1862) and on his death certificate (74yo in early 1891).

Note 3:
Sarah and Jane are much younger than the other children, though are shown as daughters of Joseph and Anne on Census records. It may be that they are actually grandchildren, given that the 1861 census also shows grandchildren (children of their daughter Emily) as their children - probably because Emily is unmarried.

Note 4:
The marriage is only shown on a paper slip index that records a marriage between "Talbut, Philip Hayes" and "Walton, Eliz", and "S. Paul, Ham'Smith 1837" but no further details.

Note 5:
See http://hidden-london.com/gazetteer/hillgate-village/

Note 6:
From http://www.archives.qld.gov.au/Researchers/CollectionsDownloads/Documents/sp7_land_orders.pdf
"A land order was legal tender for the selection and purchase of land at auction, made available under the various Queensland Land Acts in the nineteenth century. It was not an order for a particular piece of land. Therefore it is not possible to find out from land order records the locality of real property description of a piece of land selected or purchased by a particular person. The scheme was designed as a means of inducing immigrants to settle in Queensland ... The value of land orders varied. In some cases, a first land order was convertible immediately after arrival and a second land order available after a period of residence in Queensland.

Note 7:
A specific connection hasn't been located, but it is interesting that Philip was eventually living in Byfield, Qld, and his last child was born in Byfield, Northamptonshire (less than 10 miles from where he was born in Stowe, and 3 miles from where his father was born in Eydon).
