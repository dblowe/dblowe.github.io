Narrative

Story

Connecting Context: From From Clark, John (1786): Marriage shows being married to Elizabeth Jinkins, in 1806 in Wateringbury.
From sons: Clark, William Henry (1809) - death records show mother as Elizabeth Jenkins; Clark, Thomas (d. 24-1-1879 aged 69, so b. ~1809) - death index lists b in Kent, and mother as Elizabeth Jenkins

 

Elizabeth Jenkins parents, Henry Jenkins and Mary King, were married in East Malling, Kent, on 16th February 1783. Elizabeth was baptised in East Malling on 22nd April 1787, the third of at least 12 children (Samuel 1784, Sarah 1786, Elizabeth 1787, Henry 1788, Ann 1790, John 1791, Ridley 1793, Thomas 1794, Jane 1796, Charlotte 1797, William 1798, and Margaret 1800).

On 2nd March 1806, aged 18, Elizabeth married William Clark [Notes 1,2] in nearby Wateringbury. John was aged ~20, and was born in nearby Snodland. Their first child John jnr was born in East Malling 4 months after their marriage. The family then moved back to Snodland, where Elizabeth (b. 1808, d. 1808) and then William were born. The family then appears to have moved to nearby Halling [Note 3] where Mary Ann (b. 1814, d 1816), Richard (1816), and George (1818) were born. Another possible son, Thomas, might also have been born at some point in this period [Note 4].

In 1820 Elizabeth's younger brother John was caught for burglary (to a total of Â£43, of the widow Elizabeth King - possibly a relative, as John's mother was Mary King) and then sentenced to be hanged. This was subsequently changed to transportation for life to NSW [Note 5]. John was transported to Australia aboard the convict ship "Speke", arriving on 18th May, 1821, and then assigned to James Aitkinson of Oldbury, Sutton Forrest. John's family (wife Charlotte and 5 children) arrived in Jan 1827 and became established in Berrima, though John's sons held land, and hotels, around Yass, Wagga and Narrandera.

Possibly triggered by John Jenkins' transportation, sometime between 1818 and 1835 the family (John and Elizabeth Clark and at least four, and possibly five, sons) appears to have emigrated to NSW, though the records for this emigration have yet to be located, and it is not known whether they arrived together or separately, nor even definitively confirmed that John and Elizabeth emigrated to Australia [Note 6]. When they arrived in the colony it is likely that they first travelled to Sutton Forest to meet up with John.

By the mid 1830's the family was likely further south as John's son John jnr was married in Camden Forest (now Holbrook) in 1835. On 22nd July 1839 John's son William married the 15 year old Elizabeth Harris in Bowning, NSW. There are also various references to the family being in the Yass area during this time (see the information for son William, in particular). At the end of the 1830's at least three sons - William, Richard, and John - moved south into Victoria and establish squatter runs, river punts and hotels in the areas that were to subsequently become Wangaratta, Benalla, and Violet Town.

No further details can be found for John and Elizabeth, and no death record, probate, or newspaper obituary could be located. There are some newspaper references to John and Elizabeth Clarke in Sydney in 1835 and in April 1839 there is reference to a John and Elizabeth Clarke departing Sydney aboard the Louisa, heading to Port Philip, so it is possible they travelled to Melbourne via ship whilst their sons travelled inland (with stock and equipment), though this is only speculation. Irrespective of how they travelled south, it is likely that John and Elizabeth ended up in Wangaratta at some point, as a gravestone exists in Wangaratta for them both - showing John dying on November 10th 1848, aged 60 years, and Elizabeth dying less than 6 months later on April 20th 1849 aged 59 years. Their sons went on to become very successful pioneers in northern Victoria.

Note 1:
On the marriage records Elizabeth's surname is shown as Jinkins.

Note 2:
The spelling of John's surname varies significantly: Clark, Clerk, Clarke. See John's story for details.

Note 3:
This is based on the location of the baptisms, though Halling and Snodland are only 2 miles apart, so the family may have just changed their parish without moving? This also happened during the time that Thomas might have been born, and so may be connected to the difficulty in locating details on Thomas - see Note 4 below.

Note 4:
An obituary for Thomas Clark (who died in Wangaratta in 1879) describes him as being a brother of William Henry Clark, and his death index gives his mother as Elizabeth Jenkins. The obituary also states "we should guess his age at about 65", putting his birth around 1810. However no relevant baptism or birth record can be found in Halling or Snodland. A number of internet trees give his birth as 8-4-1810 in Elmstead, but this is too soon after William's birth and Elmstead is a long way from Snodland. It might be possible that his birth was unregistered, or he was an illegitimate son of John. As a possible example, there is a Thomas Wooding, b. 2-3-1806 to Sarah Wooding, no father in East Malling (i.e. same day as marriage of John to Elizabeth in East Malling)! There is no other record for a Sarah Wooding (no marriage) and no other record could be found for Thomas Wooding.
