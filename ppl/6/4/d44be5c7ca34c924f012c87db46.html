Narrative

Story

Martin Roche was born in Kilrush, County Wexford, Ireland around 1818 [Notes 1,2]. His parents were possibly Michael Roche and either Mary Carton or Ellen Jordan [Note 3]. Around 1838 (at age 20) he married Mary Carton [Note 4] and they then had their first child, Mary, in early 1939 [Note 5].

In 1840, Martin, his wife Mary, and his child Mary left Ireland. They travelled from Plymouth to Melbourne on board the Himalaya [Note 6], arriving in Melbourne on 30/9/1840 [Notes 7,8]. Martin is described as a 22 year old ploughman and his wife Mary is a 20-year old dairy maid. His daughter Mary is 1½ years old.

At the time of their arrival Victoria was barely 5 years old as a colony. Melbourne was founded (by Batman) in August 1835, with immigrants arriving over the next few years from other Australian colonies (particularly Sydney and Tasmania) – with many becoming the earliest squatters during the late 1830’s. The first colonist ships arrived at the end of October 1839, and Martin’s ship arrived less than a year later.

A letter written to a Newspaper in 1934 by Martin and Mary’s son’s John and Martin states:

"My parents left county Wexford, Ireland, the place of their birth, with one child, and embarked at Liverpool for Australia in ship Hydaspes, if I remember the name correctly [Note 9]. They arrived in Melbourne in 1840.
"After spending a week in Melbourne my father met Mr. Miller, who had taken up a station at Murrindie, a few miles from what is now the township of Yea. He remained with him as his right-hand man for twelve years. Six of our family were born on the station. Having left Mr. Miller, he bought a farm situated about 20 miles from Woodstock, and later worked bullock teams, carting from Melbourne to the goldfields."

The above is consistent with their daughter Bridget’s obituary, who refers to being born in the Strathbogie ranges on a farm [Note 10] in the area that later became Yea, with the family remaining in the area until at least 1858.

Over the 26 year period after they arrived in Australia Martin and Mary had at least 10 further children: Catherine (1841), Ellen (1843), Bridget (1846), John (1850), Sarah (1851), Margaret (1852) were all born at the Murrindie station. Thomas (~1858), William (~1858), Martin (1862) and Johanna (1866) would have been born after they moved to Woodend.

The location of the farm referred to as being owned by Mr. Miller has not been determined (much land was already taken up by 1839 [Note 11] and the family did not arrive until between 1841 and 1843). The Land Acts were passed by the Victorian Parliament in 1860, providing opportunities for taking up a ‘selection’, and it may have been this that led to the move from Yea to Kilmore. Certainly a number of his children are referred to as selectors.

Martin died in 1878 in Tatura, Victoria. When his wife Mary died in 1900 at Numurkah, 22 years later, she was then brought back to Tatura to be buried with Martin.

Note 1:
Note that in some records the surname is spelt Roach, rather than Roche.

Note 2:
The ships immigration passenger lists indicates that Martin came from Kilrush. There are multiple places named Kilrush in Ireland. Whilst there are various other references in Ancestry trees to Wexford there are no definitive sources. A search for gravestone records for “Carton” (Martin’s wife) shows them all in County Wexford, mostly in the 1800’s, with most in “Ferns Old”. Ferns is a village about 10km from Kilrush, Wexford. See http://www.from-ireland.net/free-gravestone-records/?searchterm=Carton, indicating that this is the relevant Kilrush (see http://www.kilrush-askamore.net/history.php for more details).

Note 3:
Mary Carton comes from the death certificate, but this may have been an error in writing his wife’s name. Ellen Jordan comes from a MyHeritage site, though no confirmatory sources can be found.

Note 4:
Martin’s children have birth, marriage, or death index information with a range of spellings of Mary’s name: variously as Castor, Cartnell, etc. The most common reference is “Carton”. This also seems consistent with the name written on his daughter Bridget’s marriage record

Note 5:
The Immigration record for Martin Roche indicates that his daughter Mary came with him from Ireland, arriving in Sept 1840 as a 1½ year old, hence born in early 1839.

Note 6:
See http://vic1847.comlu.com/40/him40.html#him for details of Himalaya

Note 7:
This arrival is consistent with Mary’s obituary in 1900: “ROCHE.—On the 13th April, at Numurkah, Mary, the beloved wife of the late Martin Roche, in her 81st year. Interred at Tatura. A colonist of 60 years”. This indicates she (and Martin) arrived in the colony around 1840.

Note 8:
The immigration record lists Martin and his family as “Roach” rather than “Roche”. This is however the only Martin Roach/Roche to arrive in the colony before 1850 (except for 1 convict in 1820). Note that Martin is listed as illiterate (cannot read or write) – and this might explain the confusion over the spelling of his name.

Note 9:
The ship Hydaspes did not arrive until 1852 so this is probably a misrecollection.

Note 10:
His daughter Bridget’s obituary refers to being born on “the farm of her father”.

Note 11:
See p22 of: Blanks, Harvey (1973). The Story of Yea (2001 ed.). Melbourne: The Hawthorn Press.
