Story

Connecting Context:
The baptism record for Richard Hayes Talbot (son) gives mother as Elizabeth. The 1851 UK census for Richard includes his mother Elizabeth, from Buckingham, born ~1817/18. The marriage record for Richard's father Philip lists his wife as "Walton, Eliz"

Elizabeth was born in Buckingham in 1817 to Richard Walton and Isabel Blackwell. Richard was born in Warrington, Warwickshire in 1795 and Isabel was born in Byfield, Northamptonshire in 1793. They were then married in Byfield in 1815. They had their first child (Mary) in Byfield in 1816 and then appear to have headed south, initially to Buckingham, where Elizabeth was baptised on 20th Nov 1817, and then to Isleworth, Middlesex. Richard and Isabel then had at least 7 more children, all born in Isleworth: Richard (1820), Isabella (1821), Mary Ann (1823), Louisa (1825), Eleanor (1828), Simon (1830), and William (1832). At some point prior to 1841 it is likely that Isabella died [Note 1] - though by this time Elizabeth would have been at least 15yo.

On 16/11/1836 Elizabeth and Philip Hayes Talbot (both aged ~20) had a daughter Elizabeth born on 16/11/1836 and baptised in Isleworth, London, on 26/2/1837. Philip and Elizabeth were then married in 1837 in St. Pauls, Hammersmith, though the exact date is not known [Note 2]. Philip was born in Stowe-IX-churches, only 10 miles from Byfield (and his father was from Eydon, only 3 miles from Byfield) - explaining the connection. Elizabeth's father Richard is shown in various record as a shoe maker, as is her husband Philip Talbot.

Philip and Elizabeth appear to have settled in the Hammersmith area for the next 25 years, and had another 9 children, with 7 surviving past childhood (in the following, where necessary the year of birth is estimated from census/immigration records):

- Elizabeth: b. 16/11/1836, bap. 26/2/1837 Isleworth, London
- Richard Hayes: b. ~1838; bap. 26/8/1838 at St Pauls, Hammersmith; buried 11/11/1838 at St. Pauls.
- Richard William: b. 1839; bap. 10/6/1840 at St Pauls
- Sarah Ann: b. 1842; bap. 7/7/1844 at St. Pauls
- Isabella Kitty: b. 1/3/1844; bap. 7/7/1844 at St Pauls; d. ~1855
- Eleanor: b. 21/3/1846; bap. 4/2/1848 at St. Pauls
- Edward: b. 6/1/1848; bap. 4/2/1848 at St. Pauls
- Mary Anne: b. 30/6/1851; bap. 20/7/1851 at St. Pauls
- Hannah (Anne): b. 4/3/1853; bap. 27/3/1853 at St Pauls
- Isabella: b. 12/7/1857; bap. 2/8/1857 at Byfield, Northamptonshire

Note that in several cases the death of a child (Richard, Isabella) led to a subsequent child being given the same or similar name.

The 1841 census lists Philip (20yo, Shoemaker) and Elizabeth (20yo), [Note 3] and two children, Elizabeth (4yo) and Richard (1yo), living in Lower Mall, Hammersmith, Middlesex. Ten years later, the 1851 England census shows the family living at No. 8, Brook Green Lane, Hammersmith: Philip (34yo, Shoemaker, b. Stowe) and Elizabeth (33yo, wife, b. Buckingham) and the two original children as well as 4 more children: Elizabeth (13yo, House servant), Richard (11yo, scholar), Sarah (9yo, scholar), Isabella (7yo, scholar), Eleanor (5yo, scholar), and Edward (3yo, scholar). There is also lodgers: Thomas Webb (66yo, shoemaker) and John Odell (24yo, boot closer).

In the 1861 England census the family is living at 37 Dartmoor St (now known as Hillgate Place), Kensington [Note 4], this time with Philip (44yo, Shoemaker) and Elizabeth (43yo) and just the youngest of the 6 children from 1851, but three more children: Edward (13yo), Mary (10yo), Anne (8yo) and Isabella (4yo). Their son Richard (now 21yo) is lodging nearby at 55 Dartmoor Street with the family of Joseph Harris, and both are listed as shoemakers. Sarah (19yo) is working as a servant for the Taylor family at 10 Clarendon Villas. No record has been found for Elizabeth, Isabella and Eleanor.

The following year, in 1862, Philip and Elizabeth (now 45yo and 44yo) emigrated to Australia along with all of their living children (now aged from 22yo down to 4yo) except the oldest, Elizabeth (who married and emigrated separately). The emigration was aboard the "City of Brisbane" (one of the first private immigration vessels) and Philip was employed as a cook on the ship. The ship left Gravesend on 21/2/1862, and then Plymouth on 25/2/1862. The ship travelled around southern Australia and up the east coast, arriving in Moreton Bay (Brisbane) on 28/6/1862. The ship was quite contentious for a number of reasons. Firstly, it was believed that the immigrants were headed to Maryborough and when it did not arrive there was considerable consternation.

More significantly, there were allegations of "immoralities", drunkenness, and other problems during the voyage, resulting in an inquiry being held by the Immigration Board after the ship arrived, at which Philip, Elizabeth and their son Richard were witnesses. The inquiry included allegations against Philip: "It has been clearly proved also that the cook, Talbot, whose family seem to have been highly favored by the Master - was permitted to exact fees, - to the manifest injury of those who could not, or would not, submit to such exaction" (p46). There were also allegations against Elizabeth: "Thereby further solemnly ...lure that I have seen Mrs Talbot more the once the worse for liquor - she was well known on board as a woman of low and intemperate habits - she used to frequent the Cabin called the Bush, in which two of her daughters had berths, and she was perfectly aware that three men had constant access to this cabin both night & day"  (note: presumably this is referring to Sarah, 19 y.o, and Ellen 15 y.o.). Finally there is a side note that indicates that Richard might have been employed as an assistant to the Purser: "Was there a Mr Talbot on board? There was. - A passenger? Yes. -Married or single? Single - he assisted the Purser".

By the time the family arrived in Brisbane the population of Queensland was approximately 34,300. A later obituary described Philip (and hence Elizabeth's) next few years: "… arrived in Brisbane with his family in 1862 and proceeded to Yamala, on the Peak Downs, where he remained for 12 months. He removed thence to Yaamba, where he carried on business for six years, and he then settled at St. Lawrence, where he has resided since".

Intriguingly, Elizabeth's death notice indicated that she was living at Byfield, St Lawrence (and a similar reference existed in Philip's later obituary! [Note 5]. On 26th May 1888 Elizabeth died in Byfield, QLD, aged 70 years old and was buried the following day in St Lawrence cemetery [Note 6]. Elizabeth's husband then died three years later on 16/1/1891 at the age of 74 in Springsure, Qld.
