Narrative

Story

William Charles Lowe was born in Yarrawonga, Victoria in late 1894 to Jonathan Charles Lowe and Margaret Sarah Lowe (nee Moore).

The next mention of William was as a 19 year old in May 1914, where an Albury newspaper article states: “While assisting in the removal of a tree from the Yarrawonga football ground on 14th inst., William Lowe, a young man, fell from a height of 20ft' and received a bad shaking”.

On 17th August 1915, aged 20 years and 9 months he enlisted into the 6th Battalion at Melbourne. His enlistment documentation describes him as a carpenter, with his next of kin being his father John Charles Lowe, from Yarrawonga. He is recorded as 5’ 9½”, 11st 10lbs, blue eyes and brown hair, and with scars on his left hand and chin. Key events in his military service include:

Enlisted – 17/8/1915
Embarked from Australia - 1/4/1916
Promoted to Corporal – 24/1/17
Proceeded to France – 19/6/17
Promoted to Lance Sergeant - 11/1/1918
Reprimanded and forfeited 1 day pay (absent without leave) - 26/2/1918
Wounded in action (gassed) - 1/5/1918
Discharged and rejoined his unit – 29/6/1918
Wounded in action 2nd time (gun shot wound, right thigh) - 10/8/1918
Transferred to England – 13/8/1918
Embarked from England – 17/5/1919
Disembarked Darwin, Australia – 27/7/1919
Discharged – 10/9/1919

The 6th Battalion was originally raised in 1914 as an all-volunteer force and fought at Gallipoli (taking part in the landing at Anzac Cove) and the Western Front, with a size of 700-1000 men. It was part of the AIF 2nd Brigade, which was in turn part of the 1st Division. William joined the 6th Battalion in France shortly after the Battle of Arras (Apr-May 1917) but arrived in time to participate in the battle of Menin Road (Sept 1917), near Ypres, Flanders – where the allies suffered 20,000 casualties. In March and April 1918 the 6th Battalion fought defensive actions in France, before taking part in the Allies Hundred Days Offensive, which began near Amiens on 8th August 1918. The 6th Battalion joined the offensive on the 2nd day from Villers-Bretonneux, and then William was a casualty on the 3rd day of this offensive. The result was that William spent a little over a year in the field (20/6/1917-10/8/1918).
William’s rank varied multiple times (presumably field promotions, reversions, and then confirmed promotions), and ended up being the subject of an investigation. His final rank was Lance Sergeant. William was also originally informed that he had been awarded the Military Medal whilst in France (on 26/3/1918), but a subsequent investigation indicated an error had been made and it was actually awarded to a different Soldier [Military record p16,p20]. He did receive the British War Medal and the Victory Medal [Military record p28].

Upon returning to Australia in 1919, he initially lived in Hovell Street, Yarrawonga [Military record p19], but in 1921 he married Irene May Maher in Wagga Wagga [note 1], and by Nov 1923 his address is shown as “Yam Reham”, Norman St, Sth Wagga Wagga. William and May subsequently had 5 children: Michael John (1922, died as an infant), Irene May (1923), Bridgette Mary (1926), Margaret Bernadette (1928), and John Charles (1934).

From around 1920 (i.e. very soon after returning from the war) until his death (i.e. around 32 years) he lived in Wagga Wagga and ran a business as a master builder (see the advertisement from 1939). Sometime in the late 1920’s, during the depression, he lost his house in Norman Street, and moved into a rental house in Fox Street [note 2]. The 1934 and 1937 rolls show him living back at 5 Norman Street Wagga, so he must have been able to recover the house.

During WWII he attempted to re-enlist but after his correct age was discovered he was assigned to run the canteen at the army base located at the Wagga Showgrounds, and possibly as an instructor. The 1943 electoral rolls show his wife living at 16 Docker Street, but William’s address as Albury Road [note 3].

In 1940 he contributed much of his own time to the construction of the CUSA hut at the Wagga AIF Camp. He was also mentioned in the paper later that year as the chief Marshall at a major catholic event.

In 1948 William was the driver of a utility car that ran off Mangoplah road, 17 miles from Wagga. One passenger (James Hanrahan) was killed, William suffered a fractured neck, and three others had minor injuries.

In 1949 William appeared as a witness in a court case against another Wagga builder regarding poor workmanship.

William was also a prominent Australian Rules footballer, and in later years was a noted Australian Rules referee In Wagga and district. He was also a keen bowler, and was a member of the South Wagga Bowling Club. He represented his club at Country Week on two occasions.

William died at his home (16 Docker Street, Wagga Wagga) on 4th August 1951, aged 56. He was survived by his widow (Irene May), three daughters (Irene Fabian of Sydney, and Mary and Bernadette of Wagga), one son (John of Wagga), three brothers (Thomas, Arthur and Fred, all of Yarrawonga), and two sisters (Mrs. NC Scott of Ungarie, and Mrs R McBride of Melbourne). William’s funeral was held at St Michaels Cathedral on 6th August and he was buried in Wagga.

Note 1:
Oral recollections from John Lowe (son) indicate that the move to Wagga Wagga was because William’s bride was Catholic, whereas William was Church of England, resulting in family discord.

Note 2:
Oral recollections (John Lowe), supported by the 1930 electoral roll [M3]

Note 3:
Oral recollections (John Lowe): his father lived in Docker Street all through the war. The address discrepancy may be related to his enlistment with the army. William’s obituary [N1] also mentions being an army instructor during WWII, but no relevant army record has been found.
