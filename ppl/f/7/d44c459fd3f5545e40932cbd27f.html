Narrative

Story

Connecting Context:
From Harris, Elizabeth (1824). Significant material connecting her to her parents, and especially her convict father.

 

Jonathan Harris was born around 1800, probably in either Uckfield or Mayfield, Sussex, England [Note 1]. His parents are unclear, but possibly John Harris and Philadelphia Harris (maybe Nunman or Newnham).

Jonathan married Elizabeth Baker on 17th Nov 1821 in Mayfield, Sussex and then had three children: Jonathan jnr (bap. 10 Mar 1822 in Mayfield), Elizabeth (~1824) and Henry (~1825). On 28th Feb 1825 Jonathan (aged ~24) committed burglary. He was subsequently tried (on 23 Mar 1825) at Horsham Sussex Assizes and was sentenced to be hanged but was reprieved for transportation for life to NSW. Information about the trial is given as:

"ASSI31/24 - Jonathan Harris alias Alcorn. Labourer. Committed 28th February 1825 by Richard Ironmonger Esquire. Charged on the oath of Thomas Cobram & others, with buglariously breaking and entering the dwelling house of the said Thomas Cobram at Uckfield feloniously stealing there from six linen shifts, one mahogany desk & other articles value 40/- his property.
ASSI34/52 - Charged with Burglary at Wivelsfield 24th January 1825. From John Knight and Ebenezer Knight goods valued at £18/13/-"
…
Thursday 24th March 1825 continued before the Lord Chief Baron. Jury say Guilty. No goods returned. Sentenced to be Hanged & Court judgement recorded. Also 2nd charge. Jury says Not Guilty. Acquitted and reprieved for Transportation for Life in Colony Beyond the sea".

By 26th April he was on the prison hulk York at Portsmouth. On 15th Aug 1825 he was transferred to the convict ship Marquis of Hastings, which then departed Portsmouth on 22 August 1825 with 152 male prisoners. The surgeons journal of the trip reported that scurvy occurred early in the trip, and so the ship put into Rio de Janeiro for supplies, before departing on 2nd November 1825. The ship arrived in Sydney on 3 Jan 1826. A muster was then held on board ship on 7th January by Colonial Secretary Frederick Goulburn (who was being replaced by Alexander McLeay, who also arrived on the Marquis of Hastings). The prisoners were then landed on 9th January. At the date of arriving, Australia had a European population of around 50,000 people.

The November 1828 Census lists Jonathan (possibly incorrected described as 29 years old) as having been assigned to an ex-convict, Charles Thompson, and living with Charles, his family, and a large number of other convicts at Clydesdale in the area of Sydney now called Riverstone [Note 2].

On 11 Mar 1831 Jonathan's wife Elizabeth snr and their three children, Jonathan jnr (now aged ~9), Elizabeth jnr (~7) and Henry (~6) arrived in NSW aboard the Kains (a female convict transport) [Note 3] and rejoined Jonathan snr. The family appears to have initially still been in the area around Clydesdale, as an "Exemption from Government Labor" for Jonathan on 1 June 1831 lists him living at "Windsor with his wife Elizabeth free by the Kains". They appear to have moved soon afterwards as a subsequent labor exemption, dated 2 January 1832 (and described as replacing the previous one) lists their location as Parramatta, but with this crossed out and a note indicating "to be received for Argyle (bide)? letter from Parramatta 19 Jan 1833" (i.e. a move to the Mittagong area in early 1833).

During the period from 1831, when Elizabeth arrived in NSW, and 1836, she and Jonathan had 3 more children: Sophia (b. 15/1/1832), Thomas (b. 13/7/1833) and George (b. 19/8/1835) - all baptised at Parramatta [Note 4].

In 30th Mar 1836 Elizabeth snr was travelling from Bong Bong to Campbelltown in a cart, and was killed when the cart overturned somewhere around Bringelly. The cart was being driven by "a young lad, her son" (so possibly Jonathan jnr, who would now be aged 14) and with "an infant in her arms" (so probably George, aged 7 months). The death was recorded at Cobbitty, but the burial location is unknown. It is not known how Jonathan looked after his children over the subsequent few years, though his older children would have been old enough to manage this (Jonathan 14 and Elizabeth 12).

At this point it is likely that Jonathan was employed by a William Shelley and living on a small block which was part of "Grampian Hills" owned by Shelley, and next to the Lockyerleigh property owned by Major Edmund Lockyer [Note 5].

The following year, on 7th July 1837 was gaoled in Sydney charged with robbery. The case was heard in August and Jonathan was found not guulty: "Jonathan Harris was indicted for stealing three bullocks, the property of Edmund Lockyer, of Lockyerleigh, on the 1st of May. Not Guilty". There are some references to the acquittal being because a key witness (John Stroud - an employee of Lockyer) did not appear, and was later found drowned in a creek near Jugiong. There is also unsubstantiated mention of Jonathan running a "sly grog shop" at this time.

On 19th Feb 1838, Jonathan obtained a Ticket of Leave which allowed him to remain in the district of Yass. This was later amended (in 1841) to allow him to remain in the Goulburn district.

On 22nd July 1839, Jonathan's 15 year old daughter Elizabeth married 29 year old William Clark in Bowning (though the married was registered in Gundaroo) and subsequently moved to Victoria to establish the Ovens Crossing Run (which eventually became Wangaratta).

On 12 Oct 1840, Jonathan (now aged 39) married for the second time to 21 year old Anne Grubb. The marriage certificate shows Jonathan living at Tumut River. Jonathan and Anne then had two more children (William, b. 1841 and George, b. 1843). A year later, on 1 Oct 1841, Jonathan received a conditional pardon.

Less than a year after receiving his pardon, on 22 August 1842, Jonathan was again committed to trial for robbery, this time along with his 20 year old son Jonathan jnr. A newspaper article at the time stated: "Yesterday four men were committed to take their trial for robbing one Alfred King, an old man, at Chillick's public house, on the Sydney road, of £47. The prisoners and prosecutor had come down the country from near Goulburn in company. Two of the prisoners have been sent to Hyde Park barracks to be identified; the other two Jonathan Harris and his son Jonathan, are committed. The father holds a ticket of leave. Six one pound notes, part of the property, were found concealed in one of the drays".

Both Jonathan and Jonathan jnr were initial held in Darlinghurst gaol, and were subsequently convicted on 10th Sept 1842 (the other two people mentioned were acquitted), and were sentence to 15 years and 10 years transportation to Van Dieman's Land respectively. Jonathan jnr was transported on 13th Oct 1842 on board the Waterlily, and arrived on 10th Oct. He was eventually pardoned in 1848, after serving 6 years of the 10 years sentence, and ended up in Victoria where he lived until 1881. Jonathan snr was initially transferred to Cockatoo Island where he spent a year before being transferred to Van Dieman's Land on the Sir John Byng, leaving Sydney on 14th October 1843 and arriving on 28th October.

Jonathan's second wife, Anne Harris (nee Grubb), appears to have remained in NSW and ended up remarrying in 1861 (to William Olliver) before dying the same year, though interestingly no record of a divorce can be found [Note 6]. Because of his conviction, all of Jonathan's property was forfeited, including 21 bullocks, 1 mare, 3 drays, various items and 20 acres of wheat. Anne petitioned the Governor for any surplus funds after then goods were sold. The petition mentioned that she had five children to support (possibly referring to Henry, Sophia, Thomas, George, and William).

In 1849, after 6 years in Van Dieman's Land, Jonathan was granted a Ticket of Leave. In October the following year he is listed as employed by George Halcorn (see below). On 17th May 1851 Jonathan received a pardon for the second time, after serving 9 years of a 15 year sentence.

On the 20th June 1853 Jonathan (now aged around 53, though listed as 50) married for the 3rd time to Mary Anne Squires (aged 25). Mary Anne was a convict who had arrived via the ship "Anna Maria" around two years earlier on 4th Oct 1851. Interestingly, the witnesses at the wedding were listed as George and Frances Alcorn (not Halcorn) - and 25 years earlier when Jonathan was convicted in Suusex his name was listed as "Jonathan Harris, alias Alcorn". Over the next 20 years Jonathan and Mary Anne had another 8 children (Edward 1854-1934, Walter 1855-1925, Albert 1858-1877, Frances Alice - 1860-1940, Frederick 1862-1942, John Phillip 1864-1945, Charlotte Rebecca 1866-1943, Lucy Rosetta - 1872-1961). The first two of these children were born in Tasmania, and the remainder in Victoria. This means that Jonathan had at least 16 children by three wives.

Records from this time onwards are relatively scarce. The dates and locations of the birth of the children indicates that Jonathan and Mary Anne moved to Victoria between 1855 and 1858, though no concrete details have been found regarding when they moved. The family appears to have settled fairly quickly in Benalla as the last six children were all born there, and a newspaper article at the time of his death (about general deaths related to influenza) indicates he had been resident in Benalla for 34 years (which would put his arrival at 1857).

Jonathan remained in Benalla for the rest of his life and eventually died at the age of 91, on October 4th 1891 in Garden Street, Benalla. His death certificate indicates that he died of senile decay, and that his parents were John Harris and Hilda Harris nee Nunman [Note 7] . His death certificate lists 14 of his children, but not the two children born to Anne Grubb - possibly because Jonathan had not talked about his marriage to Anne (maybe because the marriage was never annulled?) and so Mary Ann and children may not have known about this.

He was buried in Benalla on October 7th.

Note 1: Jonathan's birth is unclear. Whilst the most likely year is 1800, various records for Jonathan give his year of birth varying from 1799 (the Nov 1828 Census gave his age as 29) to 1802 (the 1831 labour exemption gave his year of birth as 1802). His native place is also variously given as Uckfield (1826 convict indents, 1841 conditional pardon), Wickfield (1831 labour exemption), and Nutfield (1842 gaol record). The most likely record is a baptism for a Jonathan Harris on 12-10-1800 in Mayfield, Sussex.

Note 2: The location of Charles Thompson's property in the 1828 Census is listed as Clydesdale, South Creek, Richmond Road, District of Bathurst the Less. This is in the area that now forms Riverstone. For details on Clydesdale see:
- http://riverstonehistoricalsociety.org.au/history.html
- p23 of http://www.bda-online.org.au/files/MC1828_Muster.pdf
- http://archiver.rootsweb.ancestry.com/th/read/AUS-NSW-HILLS-HAWKESBURY-HUNTER-VALLEY/2014-09/1410271946

Note 3: Records for the Kains list the convicts aboard (coincidentally including an Eliza Harris!) but not the free immigrants. There are various source of information (such as surgeons records but these do not mention the family). The link to this ship is however given in several sources. Various later records for Jonathan jnr (when he was convicted and sent to Tasmania) indicated he arrived aboard the Kains. And a diary by one of the seamen on the ship (Charles Picknell) mentions a free woman called Harris.

Note 4: All three children were baptised in Paramatta:
Sophia – b. 15/1/1832, bap. 26/2/1832, Parramatta (by Chas. Dickinson - from Field of Mars!)
Thomas – b. 13/7/1833, bap. 28/12/1833, Parramatta (by Rev. Robert Forrest)
George – b. 19/8/1835, bap. 9/12/1835, Parramatta (by H.H.Bobart)

Note 5:
The evidence for this is based on the link between items. The first being the 1837 court case where Jonathan was charged with stealing from Edmund Lockyer; and the second being an 1843 letter from Lockyer referring to Jonathan living at "Grampian Hills".

Note 6:
See http://www.austlii.edu.au/au/journals/UNSWLJ/2006/35.html for a discussion of bigamy in early Australia and its relationship to forceable seperation of convicts and their families.

Note 7:
The information on the death certificate was provided by Jonathan's son Frances. It is probable that Frances had only ever heard the parents names mentioned verbally by his father (who may, in turn, not have been sure), and so Jonathan's mother's name might have been any of numerous various of Hilda, as well as variations of Nunman (such as Newnham?)
